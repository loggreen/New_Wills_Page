Wills

Most people have heard of a will, but legally speaking a will is just a written document that explains what you want to happen to your property after you pass. It is a testament to your wishes, and so is also called a "testament." Someone who writes a will, then, is sometimes called a "testator."

How do I make a will valid?

There are two types of valid wills in Louisiana, olographic and notarial. Olographic wills are written, dated and signed entirely in the handwriting of the testator. It can be dated anywhere, but MUST be signed at the end. Notarial wills are signed on each page and at the end, as well as notarized and signed by two witnesses.

Who can be a witness to my will?

To qualify to be a witness to a will, the witness MUST be:
  • Sane
  • Able to see
  • Over the age of 15
  • Able to sign their name
Witnesses who receive property in the will:
If a witness receives something in a will, the will is valid but whatever they receive is invalid. But if they would be an heir without the will, then they can receive either the will provision or their inheritance, whichever is less. To see who is legally an heir, click here and read about intestacy. If a witness is married to someone in a will, then the provision for the spouse is invalid. But if the spouse would be an heir without the will, then they can receive either the will provision or their inheritance, whichever is less. To see who is legally an heir, click here and read about intestacy.
Generally, a person should not be a witness to a will that grants them property. If they do, the law will invalidate those provisions to a certain extent.

What is a legacy?

When you give someone property in your will, that is called a “legacy.” The person who receives that legacy is called a “legatee.” Legacies given to more than one person in equal shares are joint legacies. Legacies to more than one person, but assigned in distinct shares, are separate legacies. There are three types of legacies that you can leave to someone. A universal legacy gifts the entire estate, or what’s left after specific legacies have been made. It can be made to one person or jointly to several people. A general legacy is a gift of a percentage or certain proportion of the estate. A particular legacy is anything that is not universal or general, and is usually specific things being given to a person or people.

When property skips from one heir to another: lapsing

A legacy can "lapse," under certain circumstances. When a legacy lapses, it still is in effect, but instead its benefits go to a different person. This happens when any of the following occur:
  • The person to receive property dies before whoever wrote the will.
  • The person to receive property is incapable of receiving property when the person who wrote the will dies.
  • The legacy is subject to a condition, and the condition can’t happen or the person to receive property dies before the condition happens.
  • The person to receive property is declared unworthy.
  • The legacy is renounced, but only to the extent of the renunciation.
  • The legacy is declared invalid.
  • The legacy is declared null, as for example, for fraud, duress, or undue influence.

When an heir's property is split by the other heirs: accretion

Accretion takes place when a legacy lapses, a process by which someone else receives the legacy. It generally operates according to three different scenarios. When parts of a will no longer have any effect: extinguishing

Under certain circumstances, a legacy can be “extinguished,” which means it no longer has any effect. A legacy is extinguished to the extent that it involves property that it is lost or destroyed before the death of the testator. A legacy of a certain object is not extinguished when the object has been transformed into a similar object without an act of the testator.

Can I cancel my will or change parts of it? Yes you can: revocation

You can revoke an entire will if you do any of the following: (1) Physically destroy the will, or tell someone else to do it; (2) Declaring the will to be revoked with a signed and notarized writing; and (3) Identifies and clearly revokes the testament by a writing that is entirely written and signed by the testator in his own handwriting. Identify the will and revoke it in your own handwriting signed and dated at the end.
You can revoke just a part of a will if you do any of the following: (1) Declaring the part to be revoked in handwriting signed and dated at the end, or in writing signed dated and notarized with 2 witnesses and signed on each page; (2) Makes a later incompatible provision in another will. Like give a car to one person and then give it to another later; (3) Give the thing away while you are still alive; (4) Revoke the legacy in the actual original will by writing on it and then signing it; (5) If you leave something to your current spouse in a will and then divorce from them later. This happens automatically unless the testator says otherwise.
Although it sounds overcomplicated, someone can even revoke a revocation and make the original provision have effect.

General rules to remember

The language of a will always controls what will happen as long as it is clear.
When a will contains contradictory provisions, like giving the same car to two different people, the part written last will take priority.
If you owe someone money and leave them something in your will, the legacy to them does not go to paying off the debt unless you specifically say it should.
All legacies, whether particular, general, or universal, include the fruits and products attributable to the object of the legacy from the date of death. Fruits and products are things like rent from a property, or interest earned from an investment account.
Specific gifts in a will take priority over more general parts.
Any portion or part of the estate not disposed of under any of these rules or in the will is distributed by the rules of intestacy. CLICK THIS WORD

What happens if I don't write a will?

If somebody dies without a will, then their property is distributed according to automatic Louisiana laws. The process has very strict and sometimes complex rules. Learn more about intestacy here.
