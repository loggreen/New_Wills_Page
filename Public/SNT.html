Special Needs Trusts

What are the benefits of a special needs trust?

The benefits of a special needs trust are both simple and extremely important. Special needs trusts allow individuals who qualify for govenrmental benefits to keep those benefits while simultaneously having access to property and money over the maximum allowable limits mandated to retain said benefits.

What are the ideal scenarios for a special needs trust?

While a special needs trust always has the potential to be useful, it is particularly helpful whenever someone who relies on governmental benefits is shortly going to receive any type of valuable property or money. More specifically, they are commonly used when a disabled person is about to inherit assets, receive a personal injury award or settlement, or when a family member wants to give them a valuable gift. In each scenario, a special needs trust can enable a disabled individual to make use of the asset without losing their eligibility for benefits.

What can the funds in a special needs trust be spent on?

"Generally speaking, the funds in a special needs trust can be spent on anything that the disabled individual desires, as long as it does not supplant, impair or diminish any benefits or assistance by any governmental entity already being received by the beneficiary. For example, special needs trusts can spend money on vacations, vehicles, an/or entertainment for the beneficiary, however special needs trust funds cannot be spent on medical care already being covered by medicaid.

What are the types of special needs trusts?

There are only two distinct types of special needs trusts, both specifically established by federal law:
  • First-party trusts - established for the benefit of one individual
  • Third-party trusts - established for the benefit of many individuals

What are the requirements to qualify as a first-party special needs trust?

To qualify as a first-party special needs trust, a trust must:
  • Contain the assets of a diabled individual under the age of 65;
  • Be established for the benefit of that individual;
  • Be created by the individual, his parent, grandparent, legal guardian, or a court; AND
  • Contain a payback provision that when the individual dies any funds still in the trust will be used to pay back the State up to the total amount paid for medical assistance on behalf of the individual under a State plan.

What are the requirements to qualify as a third-party special needs trust?

To qualify as a third-party special needs trust, a trust must:
  • Contain the assets of a diabled individual;
  • Be created and managed by a nonprofit association or organization;
  • Maintain separate accounts for each beneficiary but pool accounts for investments;
  • Be created solely for the benefit of disabled individualsby the individuals, their parents, grandparents, legal guardians, or a court; AND
  • Contain a payback provision that when the individual dies any funds still in the trust will be used to pay back the State up to the total amount paid for medical assistance on behalf of the individual under a State plan.
